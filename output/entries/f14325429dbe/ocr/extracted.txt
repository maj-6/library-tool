This plant is not uncommon in a wild ſtate, in paſtures, 
fallow fields, waſte places, and on dry banks and walls ; 
flowering in June and July. The root and bottom leaves 
are generally formed from the fallen ſeeds before winter; and 
thus it happens in this, as in many other, caſes, that the wild 
plant is biennial ; whilſt the cultivated plant, growing from 
ſeeds ſown in the Spring, 1s annual. | | 
It is an obſervation of Linneus's, that the nodding ſpike | 
of flowers follows the courſe of the ſun, even when the ſky 
is cloudy ; pointing towards the eaſt in a morning, to the 
ſouth at noon, and weſtward in the afternoon ; in the night 
it points to the north. | 
Cattle do not eat this plant, except that ſheep ſometimes

it affords a moſt beautiful yellow dye, which is valuable for
